Why We Report IC Rather Than Backtest Returns

Alpha Factor Research Team · November 2024 · 8 min read

Contents

The Seductive Appeal of Sharpe Ratios

When evaluating a quantitative factor, the most commonly reported statistic is the Sharpe ratio of a backtest constructed over the full available history. This figure is familiar, intuitive, and easy to compare across strategies. It is also, in most contexts, deeply misleading.

The fundamental problem is selection bias. If a researcher tests one hundred candidate factor specifications on a given dataset and reports only the one with the highest Sharpe ratio, the expected value of that maximum Sharpe from noise alone is substantially above zero — even if none of the factors contain genuine predictive content. The reported Sharpe overstates the expected performance of the selected factor by a margin that grows with the number of tests conducted.

"It is no longer sufficient to merely report a Sharpe ratio; one must also account for the number of trials conducted to produce it." — Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016)

Most factor data providers do not disclose how many candidate specifications were evaluated prior to the release of their published factors. When a Sharpe of 1.4 is presented without this context, the reader has no basis for assessing whether it reflects genuine predictive content or data mining.

What the Information Coefficient Actually Measures

The information coefficient (IC) is the Spearman rank correlation between the factor signal and the subsequent realised return across assets at each rebalance period. It measures the factor's contemporaneous predictive content directly, without the confounding effects of portfolio construction rules, leverage, transaction costs, or rebalancing timing.

IC_t = rank_correlation(Signal_t, Return_{t+h})

A positive IC indicates that higher-ranked assets (by signal) tend to produce higher subsequent returns — which is precisely what we want a factor signal to achieve. The IC is also less sensitive to extreme observations, being rank-based, and does not depend on the specific portfolio construction methodology applied to the signal.

IC vs. Sharpe: A Conceptual Comparison

The Sharpe ratio of a factor backtest conflates two distinct questions: does the signal contain genuine predictive content, and does the specific portfolio constructed from that signal generate risk-adjusted returns after realistic implementation? These are both important questions, but they are different questions. Conflating them makes it difficult to isolate the source of any observed performance advantage.

IC addresses the first question. It is a signal-level statistic, not a portfolio-level statistic. A positive and statistically significant IC, computed out-of-sample, is evidence that the signal has predictive content independent of any specific portfolio construction methodology.

Limitations of IC as a Primary Metric

We do not suggest that IC is a complete measure of factor quality. IC is an average statistic and can be volatile; IC dispersion (sometimes called ICIR — the ratio of mean IC to the standard deviation of IC) is a better measure of the consistency of a signal's predictive content over time. We report ICIR alongside IC for all factors.

Additionally, IC does not account for transaction costs, market impact, or capacity constraints. A signal with a high IC but very high turnover may not be practically exploitable. We address this through our separate turnover and decay documentation.

Our Reporting Standard

For every factor we publish, we report:

The mean IC and ICIR, computed over the out-of-sample window only. The t-statistic for the mean IC, adjusted using a Bonferroni correction for the number of candidate specifications tested. The Sharpe ratio of a simple long-short portfolio constructed from the top and bottom quintiles of the signal, again computed only in the out-of-sample period. A turnover profile showing average weekly and monthly portfolio turnover. Decay curves showing how IC evolves across holding horizons from daily to annual.

We believe this reporting standard gives subscribers a more honest and actionable picture of a factor's predictive content than a headline in-sample Sharpe ratio alone.

References: Harvey, C.R., Y. Liu, and H. Zhu. "…and the Cross-Section of Expected Returns." Review of Financial Studies 29.1 (2016). Novy-Marx, R. "The Other Side of Value: The Gross Profitability Premium." Journal of Financial Economics 108.1 (2013).
